Why It Exists

The delete button lies.

When a user clicks "Delete my account," your system sends a confirmation email. The email says their data is gone. But what does your database actually do? It sets a deleted_at timestamp. The row stays. The backups stay. The replicas stay. The search index stays. The machine-learning training set stays. The confirmation email is a promise — one that the technical architecture routinely breaks.

Imagine discovering that an email address you entered into a single form seven years ago now lives in seventeen separate database tables, four backup systems, two search indices, a data warehouse, and a production machine-learning model — and that the team responsible for "deleting" it changed the deleted_at flag on exactly one of them.

— The Right to Be Forgotten, Preface

The 17-tables scenario is not a hypothetical. It is a pattern that emerges naturally from how modern applications are built: event sourcing, read replicas, ETL pipelines, caches, audit logs, and analytical stores all accumulate copies of data that no single deletion command reaches. The confirmation email goes out, the compliance checkbox gets ticked, and seventeen copies remain.

deleteceipt does not solve all of these problems. No library can. But it solves a specific, foundational one: when deletion genuinely happens — when the file bytes are gone from your tmpfs volume, when os.unlink returns, when the job directory is wiped — it produces a cryptographically signed receipt that proves the content existed and was deleted. Not a promise. Proof.

The gap between law and engineering

Privacy law has evolved faster than engineering practice. GDPR Article 17 has been in force since 2018. Most engineering teams responded by adding a deleted_at column and a cron job. That is not what the regulation requires — and courts are beginning to notice. The gap between legal compliance and cryptographic proof is exactly where deleteceipt operates.

📖
The full argument — legal, philosophical, and technical — is developed in The Right to Be Forgotten by Yoonil Choi (Kindle: B0GP35C79Q, Paperback: B0GWDJ2B71). This documentation draws on the book's core arguments.
Legal Foundation

The right to erasure

On May 13, 2014, the Court of Justice of the European Union handed down Google Spain SL, Google Inc. v. Agencia Española de Protección de Datos (Case C-131/12). A Spanish lawyer named Mario Costeja González had asked Google to delist newspaper articles about a 1998 property auction that resolved a debt he had long since paid. Google refused. The Spanish data protection authority ordered them to comply. The CJEU agreed.

The ruling did something technically modest — it required Google to delist links from search results — but legally seismic. It established that individuals have a right to have information about them made inaccessible, even when the underlying data is true, even when it was lawfully published, even when the data controller is not the original publisher. The right to be forgotten entered European law as a binding principle.

GDPR Article 17 — the six grounds

Four years later, GDPR codified this into Article 17. An individual may demand erasure when any one of six conditions holds:

  1. The data is no longer necessary for the purpose it was collected.
  2. The individual withdraws consent (where consent was the lawful basis).
  3. The individual objects under Article 21 and there are no overriding legitimate grounds.
  4. The data was unlawfully processed.
  5. Erasure is required to comply with a Union or Member State legal obligation.
  6. The data was collected in relation to services offered directly to a child.

The controller must act without undue delay. The regulation does not define this as a number of days. Courts have interpreted it as "promptly and certainly," which in practice means faster than your next weekly backup rotation.

What "erasure" means in practice

Here is where the law meets engineering, and where most implementations fail. "Erasure" under GDPR does not mean setting is_deleted = true. It means the data must be made permanently inaccessible. Backups, replicas, caches, derived datasets, and analytical stores are all in scope. The obligation follows the data, not the primary database.

⚠️
Soft deletes are not erasure. A soft-deleted record is still stored, still backed up, still queryable by anyone with database access, and still producible in discovery. GDPR enforcement actions have treated soft-deletion as non-compliance.

The global spread

The right to erasure is no longer only a European concept:

  • California (CCPA/CPRA) — Right to delete, with verification requirements and a 45-day response window.
  • Brazil (LGPD Art. 18) — Deletion of unnecessary or excessive data upon request.
  • Thailand (PDPA) — Erasure rights with controller obligations structurally similar to GDPR.
  • India (DPDPA 2023) — Erasure of personal data when the purpose of processing is fulfilled, or consent is withdrawn.

If your service is global, you are almost certainly operating under at least one of these regimes. The compliance burden is not going away. The question is how you meet it.

Why Deletion Is Hard

Why "deleted" is rarely what it seems

The filesystem makes deletion look easy. You call os.unlink(path) and the file disappears. But unlink does not erase data — it removes the directory entry that points to the inode. The inode still exists. The data blocks are still on disk, marked as available for reuse, not overwritten. On a lightly loaded system, those bytes may persist for hours or days before a new write overwrites them.

On solid-state drives, the problem is worse. SSDs use a Flash Translation Layer (FTL) that maps logical block addresses to physical NAND pages. When you overwrite a block, the FTL writes a new page and marks the old one for garbage collection. Wear-leveling algorithms deliberately spread writes across cells to extend device life — which means the "deleted" copy may be physically preserved in a different cell, untouched, until the garbage collector reaches it.

The seven layers of persistence

Beyond the filesystem, data persists through layers that each require separate, deliberate deletion:

  • Write-ahead logs (WAL) — Postgres, MySQL, SQLite all write changes to a WAL before applying them. The WAL may retain deleted data for days if archiving is enabled.
  • Read replicas — Replication lag means a deletion committed on the primary may not propagate to replicas for seconds or minutes. An application reading from a replica may serve deleted data.
  • Streaming backups — Point-in-time recovery systems snapshot the database continuously. A deletion removes the row from future snapshots but not from prior ones, which typically have retention policies measured in weeks or months.
  • CDN and proxy caches — A file served through a CDN may be cached at dozens of edge nodes globally. A deletion at the origin does not invalidate these copies unless you issue an explicit purge.
  • Search indices — Elasticsearch, OpenSearch, and similar systems build inverted indices from your data. Deletion from the primary database does not remove the document from the index.
  • ETL and data warehouses — Data pipelines that load into Snowflake, BigQuery, or Redshift typically copy at the row level. The warehouse may not support deletion, or deletion may require expensive partition-level operations.
  • Machine learning models — If a model was trained on data that included the deleted record, the record is encoded in the model weights. Technically, the only way to fully erase it is to retrain the model from scratch on a dataset that excludes the record.

The problem is not that developers are careless. The problem is that distributed systems accumulate copies of data as a byproduct of achieving durability, availability, and performance. Deletion must be explicitly designed for — it does not happen by default.

— The Right to Be Forgotten, Chapter 4

What deleteceipt can and cannot claim

A deletion receipt proves that a specific file — identified by its SHA-256 hash — was deleted from the system that issued the receipt, at the recorded timestamp. It does not prove that the data does not exist anywhere else. It does not reach backups, replicas, or downstream systems.

Used within a deletion-first architecture — where data is never written to persistent storage in the first place — a receipt can cover the full scope of the data's existence. That is the design pattern this library is built for.

Architecture

Deletion-first architecture

Deletion-first is a design philosophy: instead of building persistent storage and then trying to delete from it, you build with ephemeral storage as the default and treat persistence as the exceptional case that requires explicit justification. The difference sounds subtle. The implementation consequences are significant.

Ephemeral compute storage

The most direct implementation: run your processing jobs on tmpfs — a RAM-backed filesystem that is zeroed on process exit. Files written to tmpfs never touch disk and cannot be recovered after the process ends. On Linux, /tmp is often tmpfs by default; on production systems, you mount it explicitly: 

# Docker: mount a tmpfs volume for job working directories
docker run \
  --mount type=tmpfs,destination=/jobs,tmpfs-size=2g \
  my-processing-image

In Kubernetes, emptyDir with the memory medium achieves the same result: 

volumes:
  - name: job-scratch
    emptyDir:
      medium: Memory
      sizeLimit: 2Gi

TTL-by-default datastores

For data that must survive beyond a single process, choose stores that expire records automatically rather than requiring explicit deletion:

  • Redis SETEX — Set with expiry in seconds. The key is gone when the TTL fires, no application code required. Use this for session data, temporary tokens, processing results.
  • DynamoDB TTL — An expires_at epoch attribute causes DynamoDB to delete the item automatically, without consuming write capacity. Items are typically removed within 48 hours of expiry.
  • Kafka retention.ms — Set a retention policy at the topic level. Messages older than the retention window are deleted from all partitions automatically. Configure this to match your compliance window, not Kafka's defaults.

Data minimization

The best data to delete is data you never stored. Before writing a field, ask: is this field required for the current processing step, or am I storing it "in case it's useful later"? The latter is a liability, not an asset. GDPR Article 5(1)(c) calls this data minimization — collect only what is adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary.

In a document-processing context, this typically means:

  • Accept the file into a tmpfs staging area.
  • Extract only the fields the job requires. Discard the rest.
  • Run processing on the extracted fields.
  • Delete the original file and any intermediate representations before returning the result.
  • Issue a deletion receipt covering this lifecycle.

One-job-one-directory isolation

Isolate each processing job in its own directory. This makes deletion atomic: a single shutil.rmtree(job_dir) removes all inputs, intermediates, and outputs in one operation. There is no risk of leaving behind a stray temporary file in a shared directory. The receipt records the path and size of everything that was removed. 

import shutil, os
from pathlib import Path
from deleteceipt.receipt import compute_file_hash, issue_receipt
from datetime import datetime, timezone

def process_job(job_id: str, file_bytes: bytes, signing_key: str) -> dict:
    uploaded_at = datetime.now(timezone.utc)

    # Hash before writing — this is the commitment
    file_hash = compute_file_hash(file_bytes)

    # Isolated job directory (on tmpfs in production)
    job_dir = Path(f"/jobs/{job_id}")
    job_dir.mkdir(parents=True)
    input_path = job_dir / "input.bin"
    input_path.write_bytes(file_bytes)

    # ... run processing ...
    processing_completed_at = datetime.now(timezone.utc)

    # Collect file inventory before deletion
    files_deleted = [
        {"path": str(p.relative_to(job_dir)),
         "size_bytes": p.stat().st_size,
         "role": "input"}
        for p in job_dir.rglob("*") if p.is_file()
    ]

    # Atomic delete
    shutil.rmtree(job_dir)
    deleted_at = datetime.now(timezone.utc)

    return issue_receipt(
        job_id=job_id,
        file_hash=file_hash,
        uploaded_at=uploaded_at,
        processing_completed_at=processing_completed_at,
        deleted_at=deleted_at,
        signing_key=signing_key,
        files_deleted=files_deleted,
    )
Cryptographic Assurance

How cryptographic proof works

The library's cryptographic design rests on four primitives: SHA-256 for content commitment, HMAC-SHA256 or ECDSA for signing, canonical JSON for deterministic serialization, and SHA-256 again for hash chaining. Each is a standard, battle-tested construction. The novel part is how they are composed — specifically, the timing of the hash commitment.

SHA-256: content commitment

SHA-256 is a cryptographic hash function with two properties that matter here:

  • Determinism — the same bytes always produce the same 256-bit digest.
  • Collision resistance — it is computationally infeasible to find two different inputs with the same digest. For a 256-bit hash, the birthday bound is 2128 operations — beyond any currently feasible attack.

When you call compute_file_hash(data), you are creating a content commitment: a compact, unforgeable fingerprint of the file bytes. This fingerprint is bound into every receipt. Anyone holding the receipt and the original file can verify they match.

The Non-Obvious Insight

The pre-deletion timing is everything

A hash computed after deletion could be fabricated. A hash committed before deletion cannot.

— The Right to Be Forgotten, Chapter 6

This is the non-obvious property that distinguishes deleteceipt from a simple deletion log. Consider the naive approach: when a user requests deletion, you delete the file and then write a log entry saying it was deleted. An adversary (or a malfunctioning system) can write that log entry without ever deleting anything. The log entry proves nothing about the file's actual existence or fate.

deleteceipt inverts this. The SHA-256 hash is computed at upload time, before any deletion occurs. That hash is stored — in memory, in a database field, wherever is appropriate for your architecture. When deletion occurs, the receipt binds together the pre-computed hash, the timestamps, and the file inventory, and signs the whole package.

  • Upload time: content commitment
    file_hash = compute_file_hash(file_bytes) — the SHA-256 digest of the file bytes is computed and stored before the file is written to disk. This hash is the commitment: it proves the content existed.
  • Processing: normal work
    The job runs. Processing timestamps are recorded. The file hash sits in memory or in a job record, waiting.
  • Pre-deletion: inventory
    Before calling rmtree, enumerate every file in the job directory. Record paths, sizes, and roles (input / output / intermediate).
  • Deletion: atomic removal
    shutil.rmtree(job_dir) — the directory and all its contents are removed. The deleted_at timestamp is recorded immediately after.
  • Receipt: signed proof
    issue_receipt() assembles the payload (job_id, file_hash, timestamps, files_deleted), serializes it as canonical JSON, computes an HMAC-SHA256 (or ECDSA) signature, and returns the signed receipt dict. Store it in an append-only log.

    • The signed receipt is now a permanent, unforgeable record: this file, with this content, was processed and deleted at these times. The signature prevents the receipt from being modified after the fact. The pre-deletion hash prevents the receipt from being fabricated for a file that never existed.

    Reference

    Receipt anatomy

    A receipt is a plain JSON object. Every field is defined here. The signed receipt from issue_receipt() looks like this: 

    {
  "job_id": "b8f2a1c3-4e5d-6f7a-8b9c-0d1e2f3a4b5c",
  "file_hash_sha256": "e3b0c44298fc1c149afb4c8996fb924...",
  "uploaded_at": "2026-06-16T03:42:00.000000+00:00",
  "processing_completed_at": "2026-06-16T03:42:01.234567+00:00",
  "deleted_at": "2026-06-16T03:42:01.345678+00:00",
  "files_deleted": [
    {"path": "input.pdf", "size_bytes": 204800, "role": "input"},
    {"path": "output.txt", "size_bytes": 1024, "role": "output"}
  ],
  "server_signature": "k7mNpQr2sT4uVw8xYz0aB1cD3eF5gH6..."
}
    Field Type Description
    job_id string Your system's unique identifier for the processing job. Use a UUID or other collision-resistant ID. This is how you look up receipts.
    file_hash_sha256 string (hex) SHA-256 hex digest of the original file bytes, computed at upload time via compute_file_hash(). This is the content commitment. 64 hex characters = 256 bits.
    uploaded_at ISO 8601 When the file was received by your system. Always include timezone info. Use datetime.now(timezone.utc).
    processing_completed_at ISO 8601 When the processing job finished — output was produced, all work complete. Enables audit of processing duration for SLA compliance.
    deleted_at ISO 8601 When the files were removed — immediately after rmtree returns. The most legally significant timestamp. Record it as precisely as possible.
    files_deleted array List of {path, size_bytes, role} dicts for each file removed. role is one of: "input", "output", "intermediate". Paths are relative to the job directory.
    key_version string (optional) Identifier for the signing key used. Supports key rotation — you can verify old receipts with the key version they were signed with. Omit if you're not doing key rotation. Include it if you are.
    server_signature base64 HMAC-SHA256 over the canonical JSON of all other fields. Present in HMAC receipts. Computed after removing this field from the payload. Verification re-computes and compares.
    server_signature_ecdsa base64 (DER) ECDSA/P-256 signature over the canonical JSON. Present in ECDSA receipts. DER-encoded signature, base64-encoded. Verification uses the embedded public key.
    signing_public_key_pem string (PEM) The P-256 public key used to produce the ECDSA signature. Present in ECDSA receipts. Embedded in the receipt so it is self-contained — verifiers need no external key material.

    Canonical JSON

    Signatures are computed over the canonical form of the receipt payload. This matters because JSON serialization is not unique: key ordering, whitespace, and unicode encoding can all vary between implementations. A signature over non-canonical JSON would be meaningless — two different serializations of the same logical object would produce different signatures.

    deleteceipt's canonical form is: json.dumps(payload, sort_keys=True) — keys sorted lexicographically, no extra whitespace, UTF-8 encoded. This is deterministic across all Python versions and platforms.

    Zero Dependencies

    HMAC — symmetric signing

    HMAC-SHA256 is the default signing mechanism. It requires no third-party dependencies — only Python's standard library hmac and hashlib modules. The signing key is a server-side secret: never disclosed to clients, rotated on a schedule, and used to both sign and verify.

    ...,
    signing_key="key-v2",

    🔑
    Key management: Store signing keys in a secrets manager (AWS Secrets Manager, HashiCorp Vault, GCP Secret Manager). Never hardcode them. Rotate on a schedule. The key_version field lets you verify old receipts even after rotation.
    Third-Party Verification

    ECDSA — asymmetric signing

    HMAC-SHA256 is symmetric: verifying a receipt requires the same secret key used to sign it. If you want a third party — a regulator, an auditor, a customer — to independently verify a receipt without your involvement, HMAC fails: you would have to disclose the signing key, which would allow them to forge receipts.

    ECDSA (P-256 / secp256r1) is asymmetric: you sign with a private key that you never disclose, and anyone can verify with the corresponding public key, which you publish freely. The receipt is self-contained: the public key is embedded in signing_public_key_pem, so verifiers need no external key material.

    Tamper Evidence

    Audit log

    A signed receipt is proof of a single deletion event. A hash-chained audit log is proof that the sequence of deletion events has not been modified after the fact. Each entry includes the SHA-256 hash of the previous entry (prev_hash), forming a chain where tampering with any entry invalidates all subsequent entries.

    Entry #1 — Genesis
    seq:1 event:upload prev_hash:0000...0000 entry_hash:a1b2c3d4...
    ↓ prev_hash of next entry = entry_hash of this entry
    Entry #2
    seq:2 event:deleted prev_hash:a1b2c3d4... entry_hash:e5f6a7b8...
    ↓ tamper entry #1 → entry_hash changes → entry #2's prev_hash no longer matches
    Entry #3
    seq:3 event:download prev_hash:e5f6a7b8... entry_hash:c9d0e1f2...

    In-memory (testing)

    from deleteceipt.audit import AuditLog

log = AuditLog()  # InMemoryBackend by default

log.append_event("upload", job_id="abc", metadata={
    "filename": "document.pdf",

})

    Custom backend

    Implement the StorageBackend protocol to use any datastore. Three methods required: 

    from deleteceipt.audit import AuditLog

class MyBackend:
    def get_last(self) -> dict | None: ...
    def insert(self, entry: dict) -> None: ...
    def get_all_ordered(self) -> list[dict]: ...

log = AuditLog(backend=MyBackend())
    ⚠️
    Append-only is your responsibility. The StorageBackend protocol has no enforcement mechanism — it is an interface, not a policy. Configure your database collection to reject updates and deletes at the permission level. If an attacker (or a bug) can modify entries, the chain provides no tamper evidence.

    FastAPI integration

    A complete async FastAPI integration using the HMAC receipt and the async audit log. This covers the full lifecycle: upload, hash commitment, processing, inventory, deletion, receipt issuance, and audit logging. 

    import os, shutil, uuid
from pathlib import Path
from datetime import datetime, timezone

from fastapi import FastAPI, UploadFile
from deleteceipt.receipt import compute_file_hash, issue_receipt
from deleteceipt.audit import AsyncAuditLog, AsyncMongoBackend
import motor.motor_asyncio

app = FastAPI()
SIGNING_KEY = os.environ["DELETION_SIGNING_KEY"]
JOBS_DIR = Path("/jobs")  # mount as tmpfs

# Async audit log backed by MongoDB
_client = motor.motor_asyncio.AsyncIOMotorClient(os.environ["MONGODB_URL"])
_col = _client["mydb"]["audit_log"]
audit = AsyncAuditLog(backend=AsyncMongoBackend(_col))


@app.post("/process")
async def process_document(file: UploadFile):
    job_id = str(uuid.uuid4())
    uploaded_at = datetime.now(timezone.utc)

    # Read and hash before writing to disk
    file_bytes = await file.read()
    file_hash = compute_file_hash(file_bytes)

    await audit.append_event("upload", job_id=job_id, metadata={
        "filename": file.filename,
        "size_bytes": len(file_bytes),
        "file_hash_sha256": file_hash,
    })

    # Isolated job directory on tmpfs
    job_dir = JOBS_DIR / job_id
    job_dir.mkdir(parents=True)

    try:
        (job_dir / "input" / file.filename).write_bytes(file_bytes)
        await audit.append_event("processing_start", job_id=job_id, metadata={})

        # ... run processing ...
        result_text = "processed content"
        (job_dir / "output.txt").write_text(result_text)

        processing_completed_at = datetime.now(timezone.utc)
        await audit.append_event("processing_complete", job_id=job_id, metadata={})

        # Enumerate before delete
        files_deleted = [
            {"path": str(p.relative_to(job_dir)),
             "size_bytes": p.stat().st_size,
             "role": "input" if "input" in str(p) else "output"}
            for p in job_dir.rglob("*") if p.is_file()
        ]

        # Delete everything
        shutil.rmtree(job_dir)
        deleted_at = datetime.now(timezone.utc)

        # Issue receipt
        receipt = issue_receipt(
            job_id=job_id,
            file_hash=file_hash,
            uploaded_at=uploaded_at,
            processing_completed_at=processing_completed_at,
            deleted_at=deleted_at,
            signing_key=SIGNING_KEY,
            files_deleted=files_deleted,
        )

        await audit.append_event("deleted", job_id=job_id, metadata={
            "files_count": len(files_deleted),
            "receipt_job_id": job_id,
        })

        return {"result": result_text, "deletion_receipt": receipt}

    except Exception:
        if job_dir.exists():
            shutil.rmtree(job_dir)
        raise
    Honest Limits

    What a receipt does and does not prove

    Cryptographic receipts are precise instruments. Precision requires honesty about scope. Here is what a deleteceipt receipt proves and what it does not.

    What it proves

    • A file with SHA-256 hash file_hash_sha256 existed on the issuing system.
    • That file was deleted at deleted_at, as recorded by the issuing system.
    • The receipt payload has not been modified after signing (the signature would fail verification if it had been).
    • For ECDSA receipts: the signing server held the private key corresponding to signing_public_key_pem at signing time.
    • For chained audit logs: no entry in the chain has been modified or deleted (the chain traversal would fail if entries had been).

    What it does not prove

    • It does not prove the data does not exist elsewhere. Backups, replicas, caches, search indices, and downstream systems are all outside the scope of the receipt.
    • It does not prove the hash was computed before deletion. A malicious or buggy implementation could compute the hash after deletion and claim otherwise. The pre-deletion timing is enforced by your code, not by the receipt itself.
    • It does not prove the file existed at upload time. The hash proves content commitment — but a receipt can be constructed for any hash, including a hash of fabricated content. The receipt is only as trustworthy as the code that issues it.
    • For HMAC receipts: a verifier with the signing key can forge receipts. This is why ECDSA is preferred for third-party verification.
    • A complete audit log does not prove completeness. It proves the entries present have not been modified. It cannot prove that no entries were omitted before insertion.

    Cryptographic proof is precise, not omniscient. It proves exactly what it measures, and nothing more. The value of a receipt is proportional to the trustworthiness of the system that issues it.

    — The Right to Be Forgotten, Chapter 9
    ✅
    The right combination: Use deletion-first architecture (tmpfs, TTL-by-default, data minimization) so that the issuing system's scope covers the full lifecycle of the data. Then use deleteceipt to produce a signed receipt for that lifecycle. Together, they provide a defensible, auditable record of compliance — one that goes significantly beyond a confirmation email.
